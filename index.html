Spirit Crammed 193 Seats Per Flight. Then It Collapsed.

Airlines have been adding seats for decades. Federal data shows how far they pushed.

By Kylie Clifton

On May 2, Spirit Airlines shut down.

The ultra low-cost carrier had filed for bankruptcy twice in less than two years. About 17,000 workers lost their jobs, according to the Associated Press.

Spirit was known for packing passengers in. Federal flight data shows the average Spirit flight carried 193 seats in 2025, more than Delta, United or American.

Only Frontier, another budget carrier, fit more: 209 seats per flight.

Airlines have been adding seats for decades. Spirit and Frontier just pushed the furthest. The numbers are stark: in 1990, the average Boeing 737 carried 122 passengers. By 2025, that same aircraft family averaged 164 seats per flight.

The transformation happened across the industry. Southwest, once a low-cost pioneer, went from 130 seats per flight in 1990 to 161 in 2025. Delta climbed from 150 to 171. United from 152 to 177.

JetBlue was the only airline that bucked the trend, holding relatively steady over the years.

Air travel did not always look like this.

In 1956, the Lockheed Super Constellation carried between 47 and 106 passengers across the country. Two years later, Boeing introduced the 707, America's first successful commercial jet. Pan Am's inaugural flight carried 111 passengers from New York to Paris.

Then jets arrived.

The Super Constellation was as good as propeller travel got. Then jets arrived. And the race for more seats started. Scroll through a modern 737 MAX cabin.

Spirit and Frontier pushed the furthest.

In 2025, Frontier averaged 209 seats per flight. Spirit averaged 193. Both flew the same Airbus A320 family as other carriers, but configured them with thinner seats, less legroom and no recline.

Delta averaged 171 seats per flight. United, 177. American, 174.

Every major U.S. airline added seats over the past 35 years. JetBlue was the only exception, staying relatively flat since it launched in 2000.

The ultra low-cost model that Spirit pioneered, cramming passengers in to keep ticket prices down, proved fragile. When travel demand dropped, all those extra seats flew empty.

The aircraft tell the same story.

The Boeing 737 has been flying since 1968. It is the best-selling commercial jet ever made.

In 1990, the average 737 flight had 122 seats. By 2025, that number hit 164. That is 42 more seats, a 34 percent increase.

Some of that comes from larger 737 variants replacing older ones. The original 737-100 carried about 100 passengers. The 737 MAX 10, the latest model, can fit up to 230 in a single-class layout.

Some comes from tighter cabin layouts. The data captures both. It counts total available seats divided by total departures, meaning it reflects whatever configuration each airline chooses to fly.

The jets that vanished.

Not every Boeing has followed the same path.

The 747 averaged 326 seats per flight in 1990. By 2025, that dropped to 37. Most 747s now fly cargo, not passengers. United retired its last passenger 747 in 2017. Delta followed in 2020.

The 767 and 757 are on the way out. Both peaked in the 1990s and early 2000s. Airlines have been replacing them with more fuel-efficient models like the 787 Dreamliner.

The 737 keeps flying. And it keeps getting fuller.

Spirit is gone. Frontier is still here. The question is whether cramming more passengers onto planes remains a viable business strategy, or whether Spirit's collapse signals the limit of how far airlines can push.

Methodology

Data from the Bureau of Transportation Statistics, U.S. Department of Transportation. T-100 Domestic Segment (All Carriers), 1990-2025.

Average seats per departure calculated by dividing total available seats by total departures, grouped by airline or aircraft type and year.
